1 Introduction
CampusCart is a student-focused e-commerce platform designed to address the
inefficiencies of general marketplaces within a campus environment. The platform targets
high-frequency, low-value transactions by offering a streamlined purchasing experience,
localizedsupply, andfasterdelivery. CampusCartoperatesunderaBusiness-to-Consumer
(B2C) model, where local vendors provide products directly to student users through a
centralized platform.
The core problem lies in the mismatch between existing e-commerce systems and
student behavior. Current platforms are optimized for broad markets, resulting in longer
delivery times, higher relative costs for small orders, and low personalization. These
factors reduce the likelihood of frequent purchases among students.
CampusCart addresses this gap through three key mechanisms. First, a pre-order
system aggregates demand, allowing vendors to align supply with actual needs and reduce
waste. Second, a localized vendor network enables faster delivery, often within the same
day. Third, recommendation features improve product discovery, reducing the effort
required to complete transactions.
From a business model perspective, CampusCart operates as a volume-driven mar-
ketplace. Revenue is generated through a 10% transaction commission and a delivery
service margin. Each order produces 7,000 VND in revenue, consisting of 5,000 VND
from commission and 2,000 VND from delivery margin.
After accounting for variable costs, each transaction contributes approximately
1,000 VND toward fixed costs. With an estimated monthly fixed cost of 20 million VND,
the platform requires 20,000 orders per month to reach break-even.
Early-stage projections indicate monthly transaction volumes between 900 and
2,400 orders. This results in an expected operational deficit, which is consistent with a
validation phase focused on user acquisition rather than immediate profitability.
To bridge the gap toward sustainability, CampusCart adopts a multi-campus ex-
pansion strategy. Each campus functions as a localized demand cluster, allowing trans-
action volume to scale without proportional increases in fixed costs. As the platform
expands to 8–10 campuses, total order volume approaches the break-even threshold.
In parallel, margin per transaction improves over time as delivery efficiency in-
creases and additional revenue streams are introduced. Delivery efficiency increases
through batch processing, reducing cost per delivery. Additional revenue streams, such
as promoted listings and vendor subscriptions, further enhance profitability without in-
creasing user prices.
1.1 Problem Statement
At present, students rely on a combination of general e-commerce platforms and
local stores to fulfill their daily needs. While these options provide access to a wide range
of products, they are not specifically designed for student usage patterns.
As a result, several inefficiencies arise. Prices may vary across platforms, delivery
costs can be disproportionately high for small orders, and waiting times often extend from

two to five days. In addition, the lack of tailored recommendations makes it difficult for
users to quickly identify relevant products.
These conditions discourage users from completing small or frequent purchases,
as the effort required outweighs the perceived value of the transaction. These limitations
reduce overall convenience and negatively affect the user experience. Consequently, there
is a clear need for a more focused and optimized solution that better aligns with student
behavior.
1.2 Proposed Solution
CampusCart addresses these challenges by offering a curated platform specifically
tailored to student needs. The system incorporates a pre-order model that aggregates
demand, enabling vendors to reduce overproduction, align supply with actual demand,
and offer more competitive pricing.
Furthermore, by leveraging a network of local vendors, the platform is able to
significantly reduce delivery times, often achieving same-day or next-day fulfillment. In
parallel, the integration of AI-based recommendation features suggests products based
on user behavior, reducing the number of search interactions required to find relevant
products. This shifts the process from manual exploration to guided selection.
Together, these elements create a more efficient and user-friendly ecosystem, de-
livering lower costs, faster service, and improved customer satisfaction. This combination
positions CampusCart as a practical and scalable solution within the student market.

8.1 Margin Expansion Strategy
While the initial model relies heavily on volume, long-term sustainability requires
gradual improvement in margin per order.
This section outlines how CampusCart increases profitability without compromis-
ing user affordability.
First, commission rates are selectively adjusted. The baseline commission remains
at 10%, but premium sellers or high-demand products are subject to higher rates between
12% and 15%. This increases revenue per transaction without affecting general pricing
perception.
Second, delivery efficiency improves as order density increases. Through batch
delivery, multiple orders within the same area are grouped into a single route. This
reduces cost per delivery.
For example, if delivery cost decreases from 8,000 VND to 6,000–7,000 VND, the
net delivery margin increases from 2,000 VND to approximately 3,000–4,000 VND per
order.
Third, additionalrevenuestreamsareintroduced. Theseincludepromotedlistings,
vendor subscription plans, and targeted in-app advertising. Unlike transaction-based
revenue, these sources do not depend directly on order volume.
Over time, these improvements increase the contribution margin per order. As a
result, the platform becomes less dependent on extreme transaction volume to achieve
profitability.
In summary, the initial phase prioritizes volume and market penetration, while
later stages focus on improving unit economics and revenue diversification.

The first week is allocated to planning and architecture design. The next two
weeks focus on core development, including frontend and backend integration. The final
week is dedicated to testing and deployment.
This timeline prioritizes rapid release of a minimum viable product, enabling early
validation of user demand and operational assumptions.

12 Summary
The CampusCart system demonstrates a coherent alignment between user behav-
ior, interface design, and business model structure. The platform is specifically optimized
for high-frequency, low-value transactions, where efficiency and speed are more critical
than exploration and variety.
From a user experience perspective, the system consistently applies established
UX principles. Hick’s Law is reflected in the reduction of visible choices, particularly
in the homepage and checkout flow. By limiting options and prioritizing recommended
items, the system reduces decision time and prevents choice overload, which is known to
increase cognitive effort and delay user actions.
Cognitive Load Theory is applied through progressive information structuring.
Interfaces are designed to present only task-relevant information at each stage, avoiding
unnecessary complexity. This approach is supported by recent UX research, which shows
that excessive information increases mental processing effort and reduces task completion
efficiency .

--- Page 26 ---

Faculty of Computer Science and Engineering VNU-HCM University of Technology
Fitts’s Law is reflected in the sizing and positioning of interactive elements, espe-
cially in mobile layouts. Larger and closer interaction targets reduce selection time and
improve accuracy, which is critical for touch-based interfaces.
Compared to conventional e-commerce platforms, the system adopts a constrained
interaction model. Traditional platforms emphasize exploration, offering extensive prod-
uct catalogs and navigation layers. While this increases flexibility, it also introduces
higher cognitive load and longer decision cycles. CampusCart instead prioritizes guided
interaction and speed. This trade-off reduces browsing depth but significantly improves
efficiency for the target user group.
From a business perspective, the integration of unit economics, contribution mar-
gin tracking, and analytics dashboards strengthens decision-making capabilities. The
system connects operational actions with financial outcomes, enabling continuous opti-
mization.
Overall, the system balances usability, operational efficiency, and scalability. The
design choices are not only justified by UX theory but also aligned with the economic
constraints of a volume-driven marketplace.
